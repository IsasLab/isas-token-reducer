The lighthouse keeper had not spoken to another person in forty-one days when the boat finally rounded the headland. He watched it from the gallery, a small diesel launch riding low under crates of supplies, and felt the odd reluctance that solitude breeds. For six weeks the island had belonged to him alone: the gulls, the failing generator, the logbook he filled each night in a cramped, deliberate hand.

Now a stranger would climb the spiral stairs, ask how he had managed, and leave behind newspapers already gone stale. He descended to the jetty and caught the thrown rope out of habit. The pilot, a young woman with sunburnt forearms, grinned up at him and said the mainland had nearly forgotten the light was still manned. He said nothing, only nodded, and began carrying the crates ashore one by one, saving the mail for last.
