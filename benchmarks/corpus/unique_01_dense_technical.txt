TCP congestion control regulates how fast a sender injects data into a network it cannot directly observe. The sender maintains a congestion window, cwnd, that bounds the amount of unacknowledged data in flight. During slow start cwnd doubles every round trip, growing exponentially until it reaches a threshold or a loss is detected. After the threshold the sender enters congestion avoidance, where cwnd grows by roughly one segment per round trip, a linear probe for additional bandwidth.

A lost segment, inferred from three duplicate acknowledgements, triggers fast retransmit: the sender resends the missing segment without waiting for a timeout and halves cwnd. A timeout signals a harsher stall, so the sender collapses cwnd to one segment and restarts slow start.

Modern variants replace this loss-based signal. CUBIC grows the window as a cubic function of the time since the last reduction, spending more time near the previous maximum and probing aggressively only when the path looks clear. BBR abandons loss entirely, estimating the bottleneck bandwidth and round-trip propagation delay to pace packets at the point where throughput saturates but the queue stays short. Each design trades responsiveness against stability, and none is optimal on every path.
